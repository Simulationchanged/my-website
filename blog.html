Ultra Learning


Presentation by Scott Young

Date: 15.November.2023



The Best Way to Learn Anthing

is Scott Young's Talk on Ultralearning presents a unique approach to acquiring skills and knowledge.


Scott Young's approach to learning, known as Ultralearning, is an intense, self-directed method that empowers learners to take control of their educational journey. This strategy is particularly effective in situations where traditional learning approaches might fall short. Ultralearning is defined by its intentional, focused nature, enabling individuals to tailor their learning experience to their specific goals and needs.


At the heart of Ultralearning lies the concept of Metalearning, where understanding the core elements of what, why, and how forms the foundation of the learning project. This involves a deep dive into the specifics of what you're trying to learn, why it's important, and how best to approach it. This foundational step is crucial for setting the direction and purpose of the learning journey.


A key aspect of Ultralearning is the emphasis on focus. Distractions are the enemy of fast and effective learning. To combat this, Young suggests acknowledging the triggers of procrastination and starting with small, manageable learning sessions. This approach helps in gradually building up the concentration and commitment required for deeper learning.


Directness in learning is another principle that Young emphasizes. Instead of taking easy or indirect routes, Ultralearning encourages learners to directly engage with the skills or knowledge they aim to acquire. For example, if someone is learning a new language, rather than just studying grammar rules, they would immerse themselves in conversations using that language.


Drilling down into specific areas of weakness is also a critical component. This approach, inspired by the concept of deliberate practice, focuses on identifying and addressing the most challenging aspects of what you're learning. Instead of broadly improving strengths, Ultralearning targets the areas that most hinder progress.


Retrieval practice is another technique used in Ultralearning. It involves consistently testing oneself to ensure that information is not just read but truly learned and remembered. This practice helps in overcoming the illusion of knowledge, ensuring that learners can recall information when needed.


Feedback loops are integral to Ultralearning. These loops allow learners to immediately understand the correctness of their answers or approaches, enabling quick corrections and adjustments. This process contrasts with traditional educational systems where feedback may be delayed or not directly tied to the learning objective.


Retention of knowledge, a challenge in rapid learning scenarios, is addressed through techniques like spaced repetition. This method involves revisiting information at strategic intervals, just when you're about to forget it, thereby strengthening memory and retention.


As learners progress, developing intuition becomes vital. This comes from deep engagement with the subject matter and is accelerated by techniques like the Feynman Technique, which involves simplifying and explaining concepts in layman's terms. This process not only aids in understanding but also in internalizing complex ideas.


Finally, Ultralearning involves a degree of personalization through experimentation. As learners become more adept, they are encouraged to experiment with different methods, integrating various techniques and approaches to enhance efficiency and effectiveness. This final principle signifies a transition from following structured learning paths to creating personalized strategies that align with individual learning styles and goals.


In summary, Scott Young's Ultralearning presents a comprehensive, adaptable framework for learning that is intensive, self-directed, and highly effective. It empowers learners to take charge of their educational journey, emphasizing the importance of focus, direct engagement, targeted practice, consistent feedback, and the creative application of knowledge.

Inventing on Principles


Presentation by Bret Victor

Date: 31.October.2023



Inventing on Principle is a talk by Bret Victor in which he elaborates on the idea that invention should be driven by a deep foundational principle â€” an unwavering belief that guides an individual's work.
Imagine, if you will, a compass in the hands of an explorer. While the terrain they navigate is unknown and fraught with challenges, the compass provides a reliable direction, ensuring that every step taken aligns with a predetermined path. Similarly, Bret Victor argues that innovators and creators need such a compass in the form of a guiding principle. This principle becomes the core belief, fueling every innovation or invention they produce.
One of Victor's foundational principles is that creators should have an immediate and intimate connection with what they create. To elaborate, think of a painter who, with every brushstroke on canvas, sees the colors transform before their eyes. There's no delay; there's immediate feedback. Now, apply this to the world of software development. Traditionally, writing code is like painting blindfolded. Developers write lines of complex code and then run the program to see if it works. There's a delay, often leading to inefficiencies and frustration.

Using this principle, Victor showcases several examples:


Code and Output Side-by-Side: Imagine a split-screen setup. On one side, you have the code you're writing, and on the other, you see its real-time execution. As you modify the code, the changes manifest instantly. This not only saves time but also offers a deeper understanding of how changes affect outcomes.


Dynamic Drawing Tool: Victor demos a tool where, as you draw a design or sketch, parameters (like angles, lengths) can be adjusted in real-time. If drawing a tree, for instance, changing a parameter could instantly show more branches, adjust their angle, or change their length.


Game Parameter Adjustment: For game developers, Victor presents a platform where they can play a game and adjust parameters (like gravity, jump height, speed) in real-time. It's like playing a video game and having a slider that lets you adjust the game's physics on-the-fly.


All these examples resonate with his principle of immediate feedback and connectivity between creation and creator.


But beyond these applications, Victor's broader message is about recognizing one's guiding principle and committing to it. Just as civil rights leaders were driven by deep principles about equality and justice, inventors should also be motivated by principles that they believe can change the world. It's not about inventing for the sake of invention but about ensuring that every creation aligns with a deeper purpose.


In conclusion, Bret Victor's "Inventing on Principle" is a clarion call for purpose-driven innovation. Just as a compass guides an explorer, a deep-rooted principle should guide inventors. Through immediate feedback tools, Victor showcases the transformative power of staying true to one's guiding principle, urging all creators to find and commit to theirs.

A More Creative Brain


Written by James Clear

Date: 30.October.2023



The essence of creative thinking is the ability to connect old ideas in novel ways. This article stresses the universality of the creative process and underscores its significance in addressing everyday challenges. Mastering creative thinking can dramatically benefit personal and professional problem-solving.
A historic illustration of this process is the predicament newspapers faced in the 1870s. Readers sought more photographs, but printing these images swiftly and economically was an obstacle. Traditional methods like photoengraving were both slow and costly. The solution came from Frederic Eugene Ives, a pioneer in photography. His journey to revolutionize the printing technique provides a practical model of the creative process.
Ives' story starts in Ithaca, New York, as a printerâ€™s apprentice. His experiences in the printing realm and later, managing a photographic lab at Cornell University, immersed him in the challenges of photography and printing. One morning in 1881, after considerable reflection on a better printing method, Ives experienced a sudden revelation about a new technique. This led to the development of the "halftone printing" method, a breakthrough that reduced image printing costs fifteen-fold and remained the industry standard for nearly eight decades.
James Webb Young, an advertising executive, later distilled the creative process into five stages in 1940. His model proposes that innovative thinking isn't about conjuring something entirely new but about uniquely combining existing elements. Central to this is the ability to discern relationships between disparate ideas. The stages are:


  • gather new Material:

    • Engage in both task-specific and general learning. Absorb a broad range of concepts.

  • mentally process this information:

    • Meticulously examine and ponder over the accumulated knowledge, playing around with various idea combinations.

  • step back from the problem:

    • Deliberately divert your attention from the challenge, engaging in other stimulating activities.

  • wait for an insight or solution:

    • After a break, insights will resurface spontaneously, often with renewed vigor.

  • refine the idea based on feedback:

    • Once conceived, expose the idea to external criticism and evolve it accordingly.

In summary, creativity is about linking existing concepts in innovative ways.
